About

The person who builds the relationship, not just closes it.

I've spent six years turning cold conversations into long-term partnerships. I have closed deals with organizations like Mercedes F1 Team, Sony, and 100 Thieves, and have built the systems that keep those relationships growing well after the ink dries.

The Situation

One category to build. One market to break into.

In esports, Blinkfire had only a handful of clients when I joined, including the team I represented before joining the company. Budgets were unpredictable, decision-makers were scattered across teams, leagues, and brand partners, and there was no established buying pattern to lean on. My job was to build that market from the ground up.

In motorsports, the situation was different. Blinkfire already had a foothold, but needed to take another step. The challenge there wasn't creating demand, it was earning trust and access inside a market that already had established relationships and a high bar for credibility.

My Role

Grew a new segment. Landed anchor names in an established one.

I owned the complete sales cycle for esports and motorsports accounts, from first outreach through negotiation and renewal, carrying a $250K annual net-new quota. In esports, I significantly grew Blinkfire's client base, closing organizations including 100 Thieves, FaZe Clan, and United Talent Agency. In motorsports, I added marquee names to an already strong roster, closing Mercedes F1 Team and a league-level deal with INDYCAR.

I built my pipeline in Pipedrive, sourced and qualified leads through LinkedIn Sales Navigator, and used Earned Media Valuation (EMV) data to give prospects concrete, measurable proof of sponsorship value instead of relying on projections alone.

Pipedrive LinkedIn Sales Navigator Earned Media Valuation (EMV) HubSpot
The Result

171% of quota. Growth in a new category and a mature one.

Over three years I closed $945K in net-new business and managed $730K in renewals, hitting 171% of quota in 2022 and 118% in 2023. That total reflected two different kinds of wins: expanding Blinkfire's presence in an undefined market, and proving the company could compete for and retain some of the biggest names in an established one.

I built the team's sponsorship program from the ground up: identifying and prioritizing prospective partners, developing pricing and packaging for sponsorship assets that didn't have an established market rate, and managing partnership activations as the client's main point of contact. I used Blinkfire Analytics to create data-driven sponsorship packages and improve transparency with current partners.

I worked directly with brand partners to explain the value of esports sponsorship to companies that had never bought into gaming before, translating an unfamiliar category into terms a traditional sponsor would recognize and trust.

The Situation

A local connection, and no category playbook to follow.

Minnesota Røkkr's whole positioning in the Call of Duty League centered on a local, hometown identity. A coworker's personal connection to Waconia Brewing Company, a craft brewery in the Twin Cities suburbs, opened the door to something almost no esports organization had done at the time: a custom-branded alcohol sponsorship.

There was no established path to follow. Alcohol sponsorship was a new category for an esports team, which meant navigating legal requirements with no internal precedent, while also introducing the brewery's staff to an industry most of them had never heard of.

My Role

Ran point on the partnership from pitch through league approval.

I led the relationship with Waconia Brewing from the initial conversation through a signed, approved deal. The hardest part was securing league-level approval through Activision, which controlled all use of Call of Duty League marks. Over the winter of 2020 and into 2021, I worked directly with our art director and Activision to get the branding approved, while also building buy-in internally across the Røkkr staff.

In the spring of 2021, I helped run a taste test at the brewery with staff to select the final beer style and flavor. By the time I moved to my next role that May, the recipe, the branding, and the league approval were all locked and ready for production.

Partnership Development Brand Approval Process Creative Direction Cross-Functional Collaboration
The Result

Final Circle Shandy: a first-of-its-kind sponsorship built from scratch.

Named after the popular Call of Duty: Warzone battle royale game, Final Circle Shandy launched into Waconia Brewing's taproom and regional liquor stores that summer, sold in custom-designed cans and 4-pack boxes we developed with our art director. The partnership later grew further, with the brewery hosting watch parties for Røkkr matches, extending the relationship well beyond the original product.

It remains one of the more unique partnerships I've built: a physical, retail product born from a local relationship, built without a category precedent to follow, and delivered through a rights-holder approval process most sales roles never have to navigate.

The Situation

A deal that started on day two, for something we'd never sold before.

Two days into my role at Stream Hatchet, I flew to TwitchCon San Diego 2024 and met someone from Sony's Strategic Partnerships department. Sony wanted a custom market sizing project to better understand the live streaming market and where their brand fit within it. We knew it was something we could do, but it was a request that didn't map to anything Stream Hatchet had sold before.

It took about three months of relationship-building and scoping to get the deal signed. When it closed at $60K, it became Stream Hatchet's largest consulting engagement to date. Then came the harder part: Stream Hatchet's infrastructure was built for recurring ARR contracts supported by a customer success team, not a one-off custom research project. There was no existing process for actually delivering something like this.

My Role

No playbook existed, so I built one.

I owned the project end-to-end: scoping the deliverable with Sony, closing the deal, then running project management, execution, delivery, and the final presentation myself. That meant pulling together a working team across marketing, customer success, data analysts, engineers, and designers — a group that didn't normally work together on a single deliverable — and keeping the project moving without an existing process to fall back on.

The final product was a roughly 160-page presentation breaking down the scale and structure of the live streaming and gaming market, which I personally presented to senior Sony executives based in Tokyo, entirely over video call.

Deal Closing Project Management Cross-Functional Leadership Executive Presentation
The Result

Glowing feedback, and a repeatable model for what came next.

The project landed. Sony's team gave glowing feedback, with several executives saying they hadn't grasped the true scale of the live streaming and gaming industry (or how relevant their own brand already was within it) until seeing the data laid out. Beyond the revenue, the deal proved out a repeatable model for how Stream Hatchet could scope, sell, and deliver custom one-off engagements going forward, work that started with a conversation on my second day on the job.
